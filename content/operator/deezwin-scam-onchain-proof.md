## How to Verify a Crypto Casino Scam On-Chain

Traditional scam detection tools like Gridinsoft, URLVoid, or ScamAdviser scan domain registration data, SSL certificates, and threat intelligence feeds. These are useful for an initial screen, but they can't tell you the most important thing: whether the casino actually holds the funds it claims to hold.

For crypto-native casinos, on-chain verification is more powerful because every transaction is public. Here's what to check:

1. **Wallet mapping**: A legitimate crypto casino should have publicly identifiable deposit and withdrawal wallets. If an operator doesn't publish wallet addresses — or its wallets show zero meaningful activity — that's a major red flag.

2. **Proof of reserves**: Can the platform prove it holds enough to cover user balances? Proof of reserves compares on-chain wallet balances against known user liabilities. An operator with no proof of reserves, or one whose coverage ratio is declining, is a serious warning sign.

3. **Wash trading detection**: Some scam sites fabricate volume by cycling funds between internal hot wallets. By tracking 11+ blockchains and filtering out internal wallet-to-wallet transfers, you can separate genuine external deposits from manufactured wash trading.

4. **Independent third-party ratings**: Check whether the casino appears on platforms like Casino.guru, AskGamblers, Casino.org, or Trustpilot. A brand-new casino with zero presence on any of these — or only generic five-star reviews — should be treated with suspicion.
